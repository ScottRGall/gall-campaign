Session 4 Player Recap

The East Tunnels of Greyvein

I knew something was wrong the moment he looked at my necklace. Most people see shells and think of the ocean. Calm tides. Gentle waves. Stories. Mayor Dorrin Flintkettle saw something else. He watched me from across the room like he was measuring something… not my strength, not my skill—something deeper. When he finally approached, his words were blunt, but his eyes were careful. “You look like you know your way around water.” I didn’t answer him right away. Because the truth is… I do and I don’t trust it either. “What do you mean by that?” I asked. He didn’t hesitate. “Water doesn’t forgive.”

That sat with me longer than I liked.

He told us about the east tunnels—old, abandoned, reopened too soon. Miners had gone in and come back… unsettled. There was water where there shouldn’t be water. Echoes lingered too long, like the stone had forgotten how to hold sound. Tools vibrated in their hands and a sickness no one could name.  I didn’t like any of it. Not the water, not the echoes, not the feeling that something unseen was already moving. The mayor felt like the miners are just goldbricking. Still… we agreed for a price: 120 silver a day for the group. 

The Next Day…

We met the following morning with Foreman Joseph.  He gave us the map and sent us in. The tunnels were… good - solid and reassuring. Dwarven work always is. For a while, I almost convinced myself this was nothing. Then came the dripping. Slow and steady and too rhythmic to ignore. I found myself listening to it more than I should have… trying to place it. Ocean? No. Something else…something trapped. At the sixty feet mark, the stone changed.  Cracks split the walls—sharp, fresh, unnatural, but dry. That’s what bothered me most. If water was coming in… it would be there. Seeping. Spreading. But this? This was like the stone had been forced open from the inside and whatever had done it, hadn’t brought water with it. The rumble didn’t come from ahead of us. It came from everywhere. I felt it before I heard it. Through something deeper and then the walls broke.

Three of them came through the stone like it wasn’t there. They weren’t animals. They were something else. Something that didn’t belong in the natural order of things. Not knowing what they are called, we decided to call them Murder Ticks. They trapped us in the tunnel immediately—no room to move, no space to breathe.

And then the larger one came: The Monster Momma Murder Tick!  Yikes! The fight was chaos: too close, too loud, too much scraping and cracking and impact. One of the murder ticks refused to fall. We hit it again and again and again and it barely gave out. It became something else then, not a battle but a gradual wearing down. The largest of the ticks—the one we’d labeled 'Monster Momma'—ignored us entirely for a moment, focusing its horrific mandibles on Severance. It hammered against his shield and armor with such vicious, relentless force that the metallic ringing was deafening, a desperate drumbeat drowning out our cries. He was a piece of sporting equipment being used for target practice, standing firm while the creature tried to turn him into a dented can. Finally it stopped moving. We decided to head back to the surface and report to the Foreman and the Mayor.

The Magic Was Wrong

I reached for my magic without thinking. Healing has always felt like balance like the tide but this time, it burned. Not painfully—but warm in a way that didn’t belong. And there was light… purple. Not anything I’ve ever called upon. It flickered through every spell I cast and then I saw it… the creatures turned toward it and suddenly I didn’t feel like a healer. I felt like a beacon.

Holding the Line

The fight was chaos: too close, too loud, too much scraping and cracking and impact. One of the murder ticks refused to fall. We hit it again and again and again and it barely gave out. It became something else then, not a battle but a gradual wearing down. Finally it stopped moving. We decided to head back to the surface and let the Foreman and the Mayor.

Foreman Joseph believed us.  He rang the bell, and the mine changed in an instant. Dwarves moved quickly. Efficiently. Without panic—but without hesitation either.  They knew danger when they heard it. He hadn’t expected to see us again so soon we could tell. But when we told him…when we showed him… he understood. Faedove did most of the talking—smooth, quick, convincing. This wasn’t water creeping into his mine, this was something else entirely.

The Next Step

That night, we headed back to the Lantern’s Rest where our room and board was compted for the night.  Later, the Mayor brought us three dwarves; Torvi, Cargun and Dorne. They knew the tunnels but they were afraid.  Faedove did some fast talking and convinced them to join us after explaining she had a spell that would offer them protection.

The mayor left, the dwarves finally shuffled out to get what little sleep they could, and the tavern quieted. I watched Faedove settle into her bedroll, still fiddling with the arcane components for the spell she’d promised. The purple light was gone now, thankfully, but the warmth—that wrong heat—still lingered in my bones. I slipped off my shell necklace and held it tight. It felt cold against my palm, a comforting reminder of the ocean I knew, the balance I trusted. Tomorrow, we will go back in. Back into the earth that had tried to swallow us whole. Back into the dark where the stone screamed and the magic was purple. I didn't know what the mayor was measuring when he looked at me.  Tomorrow would be worse, but tonight, I was safe. And with that thought, I finally slept.
